Analyzing Cumulative Weight \(f(x)\) and Growth Rate Derivative \(f'(x)\) in Malaysian Closed-House Systems

Target Context: DVS Malaysia & MARDI Standards

📖 The Bioscience Scenario

In intensive broiler poultry production (e.g., standard Cobb 500 or Ross 308 breeds), growth is non-linear and conforms to a sigmoidal (S-shaped) logistic curve. Understanding the instantaneous rate of change—the First Derivative \(f'(x)\)—is critical.

The derivative represents the daily average weight gain (grams/day). In animal husbandry, identifying when the growth rate peaks is vital. Once the derivative peaks and begins to decline, the broiler's Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR) decreases. Feeding the bird past this point increases expenses rapidly, as feed represents nearly 70%+ of total production costs in Malaysia.

📊 Mathematical Growth Model

Cumulative Growth \(f(x)\):
$$f(x) = \frac{W_{max}}{1 + \beta \cdot e^{-k \cdot \eta \cdot x}}$$
Derivative \(f'(x)\) (Daily Growth Rate):
$$f'(x) = \frac{W_{max} \cdot \beta \cdot (k \cdot \eta) \cdot e^{-k \cdot \eta \cdot x}}{(1 + \beta \cdot e^{-k \cdot \eta \cdot x})^2}$$
\(x\) = Day of Growth cycle (0 to 42)
\(W_{max}\) = Target asymptotic weight (g)
\(\beta\) = Hatch scaling parameter (fixed at 55)
\(k\) = Biological growth coefficient
\(\eta\) = Housing Efficiency Index (0.7 to 1.15)

Deep-Dive: How Calculus Solves Poultry Economics

1. The Biological Peak

The peak of the derivative curve \(f'(x)\) occurs at the inflection point of the cumulative weight curve \(f(x)\). Mathematically, this is where the second derivative equals zero: \(f''(x) = 0\). Practically, this marks the maximum possible growth efficiency of the broiler. After this day, the rate of weight accumulation slows down, and daily energy intake goes more toward body maintenance than tissue growth.

2. The Microclimate Equation

In tropical Malaysia, temperatures regularly exceed 32°C. In the simulator, the Housing Efficiency Index (\(\eta\)) represents the microclimate parameter. In an open-house system (\(\eta \approx 0.75\)), birds suffer heat stress, slowing metabolic enzyme processes, shifting the inflection point to the right, and depressing the peak derivative value. Setting \(\eta\) to 1.15 reflects high-speed tunnel houses that mitigate heat-stress.

3. Deciding the Harvest Date

Broilers are usually harvested in Malaysia between days 35 to 42. By cross-referencing feed cost per day (which is linear-increasing) against cumulative weight value (which is sigmoidal), farmers use the derivative curve to locate the point where profit margins are maximized. Harvesting on day 35 (approx 1.8-2.1kg) meets standard wet-market requirements, while day 42 caters to downstream premium portion processors.
